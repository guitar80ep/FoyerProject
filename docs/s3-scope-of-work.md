### §3.A · Low-Porosity Porcelain Tile Cladding

| Attribute     | Value |
|---------------|-------|
| Description   | Replace failed travertine with rectified porcelain tile on all wall surfaces and parapet faces |
| Key Specs     | Tile: rectified porcelain, full-body, ≤0.5% absorption (ANSI A137.1 Group V, Impervious), PEI IV or V, matte/honed finish, frost-resistant (ANSI A137.1 F-rated). Format: 24″×24″ or 24″×48″ walls; 12″×24″ planters. Style: travertine-look, warm buff/ivory/gray. Thinset: ANSI A118.4 polymer-modified, 95% min. back-butter coverage. Grout: epoxy, non-porous, salt-resistant; sanded ≥1/8″ joints, unsanded <1/8″. Movement joints: TCNA EJ171, color-matched marine silicone at inside corners, wall-to-floor transitions, max 12 ft o.c. |
| Standards     | ANSI A108, ANSI A118.4, ANSI A137.1, ASTM C920 Class 50 (silicone), TCNA EJ171 → see §10 |
| Status        | [TBD] — Specific tile style/color pending selection and Planning Dept. Condition 23 confirmation |

> **Rationale:** Porcelain at ≤0.5% absorption eliminates the fundamental porosity failure of travertine. Visually identical to natural stone in modern large-format tiles. Sealing not required on tile face (grout joints still need sealer). Available in travertine-look finishes that satisfy Planning Condition 23 warm stone aesthetics. Alternatives rejected: sealed natural stone (sealing degrades in coastal UV/salt); ceramic tile (lower durability, higher absorption); stucco (different aesthetic, not consistent with original approval).

> **Rationale (epoxy grout):** Epoxy grout is non-porous and impervious to salt water. Standard Portland-based grout absorbs moisture and salt ions, leading to spalling and cracking within a few years at coastal locations. Working time is approximately 20–30 minutes at typical SoCal temperatures — mix only what can be placed and cleaned in that window. Remove from tile face before it skins over. Alternatives rejected: unsanded cementitious (absorptive, salt-vulnerable); polymer-modified cementitious (acceptable but still absorptive vs. epoxy).

### §3.B · Full Tear-Down, Framing Repair & Wall Assembly Rebuild

| Attribute     | Value |
|---------------|-------|
| Description   | Demo to structural frame. SE assesses first. Replace rotted sheathing, damaged sill plates, wet framing. Rebuild wall assembly: sheathing → WRB → drainage mat → backer board → thinset → tile. 316 SS weep screed at all wall bases. |
| Key Specs     | Sill plates: PT ACQ or CA-B rated lumber. All framing within 6″ of grade/concrete must be PT. Replace any member with visible rot, soft spots, or >25% section loss — full length, no splice repair. Borate treatment (Tim-bor or equiv.) on all retained framing before sheathing. Sheathing: 5/8″ CDX plywood, nailed per CBC shear wall schedule. WRB: Henry Blueskin VP100 or equiv., shingle-lapped min. 6″. Drainage mat: Benjamin Obdyke Slicker Classic, 3/8″ gap. Backer board: HardieBacker ½″ (ASTM C1325), 316 SS screws per manufacturer schedule. Weep screed: 316 SS V-notch, min. 3/4″ ground face, min. 2″ above finished paving, 316 SS fasteners at 6″ o.c. max, 6″ min. laps sealed with flashing tape. |
| Standards     | AWC NDS 2018 (member sizing), CBC 2022 Chapter 16 (SDC D), CBC 2022 Chapter 23 (shear wall schedule), ASTM C1325 (backer board) → see §10 |
| Status        | [PENDING-SE] — Full framing scope cannot be defined until SE report is in hand |

> **Rationale:** The original travertine was set directly against the substrate with no WRB, no drainage mat, and no weep screed. Any water that penetrated the stone had nowhere to go and accumulated against the wood framing. The new wall assembly creates a complete drainage plane: the WRB blocks bulk water, the drainage mat creates an air gap for water to channel downward, and the weep screed provides a defined exit point at the wall base. This is the critical assembly correction that prevents the original failure mode from recurring.

> **Rationale (316 SS requirement):** All exterior metal fasteners, screws, clips, and weep screeds in this project must be 316 stainless steel. 316 SS contains molybdenum which provides superior resistance to chloride (salt) corrosion. 304 SS — the more common grade — pits and corrodes in coastal salt air within 2–5 years. "SS" or "stainless" without a grade specification almost always means 304. Always verify the grade before accepting any stainless steel product for this project.

> **Rationale (system change from standing seam metal):** The original specification (McClelroy Maxima 3″ structural standing seam) delivered the longest raw service life (40–50 years) but required a rented mechanical seamer, manufacturer-specific clips with FM/UL confirmation, a 10-item pre-order checklist with McElroy rep coordination, and precise seam engagement technique — all factors that increase DIY error risk on a first-time installation. The Polyglass three-ply self-adhered SBS system provides a ~320-mil fully bonded membrane with 20–30 year base life, extendable to 30–40+ years with periodic silicone recoat (PolyBrite 90.1/95.1, ~half-day job on 97 sq ft every 10–15 years). Installation is peel-and-stick with no torch, no hot asphalt, no mechanical seamer, and no specialized equipment beyond a roller. The owner's main roof already uses the Polyglass ADESO product family, so the material handling is familiar, the supplier relationship exists, and the single-manufacturer membrane + coating warranty path is available. Alternatives rejected: McClelroy Maxima standing seam (excessive DIY complexity, specialized equipment, long lead time); EPDM rubber (no existing supplier relationship, granule-surfaced SBS provides better puncture and UV resistance); TPO/PVC (hot-air welding required for reliable seams); liquid-applied membrane (10–15 years standalone, film thickness control difficult for DIY).

> **Rationale (slope direction):** Minimum code slope is ¼″:12″ (~2.5″ rise over 122″). Owner elected ½″:12″ (5″ rise) for margin. Draining toward street puts the rain chain at the most visible and accessible location as an entry design feature. Building side being higher keeps debris-blockage ponding away from the main structure.

> **Rationale (through-wall parapet drain vs. gutter):** Eliminating the gutter removes ~230″ of half-round gutter, ~24–28 hangers, and the gutter slope/alignment task. A through-wall parapet drain is a simpler, more reliable solution for a parapet-enclosed roof — water exits at the parapet base rather than overtopping an edge. The rain chain connects directly to the 3″ no-hub spout, a cleaner detail than a gutter outlet adapter.

> **Rationale (integrated overflow — single-unit proposal):** CBC §1502.2 requires secondary overflow drainage for parapet-enclosed roofs. The SPDSRCB3NH integrates the overflow 2″ above the primary inlet in a single through-wall body, eliminating a second parapet penetration and a second membrane flashing detail. Primary and overflow share the single 3″ no-hub spout. This is the simpler installation and has been proposed to Redondo Beach B&S for confirmation. If the inspector requires a separate visible overflow discharge (free-draining through the parapet face), the fallback is the Thunderbird SS Separate Parapet Overflow Drain as a second unit — which also changes the parapet framing rough opening count → see §6 Q17.

> **Rationale (drain material — 316 SS):** Marine-grade 316 SS body chosen for maximum corrosion resistance in coastal salt-air, consistent with the project-wide 316 SS hardware standard. The sandblasted SS flashing flange bonds directly to SBS membrane — no compatibility concerns. Black powder-coated cast aluminum dome strainer is the only visible face from street level and matches the parapet flashing finish. TPO/PVC-clad variant explicitly rejected — heat-weld requirement is incompatible with self-adhered SBS system.

> **Rationale (rain chain material):** Tapered Square Cups uses galvanized iron cups with SS connecting wires, finished in black powder coat. This is not 316 SS, but the chain hangs free in air with no bimetal joint under load. Powder coat is the primary corrosion barrier; zinc galvanizing is secondary. Inspect annually and re-coat if chipping is observed. The tapered square cup geometry and custom 13 ft length are not available in a 316 SS option from this manufacturer.

### §3.D · Maximo Thermo Radiata Soffit

| Attribute     | Value |
|---------------|-------|
| Description   | Thermally modified radiata pine on archway soffit. 316 SS hidden clip system. PT framing with 316 SS fasteners. |
| Key Specs     | Board: Maximo Thermo Radiata pine, 5/4″ or 1″ nominal T&G or shiplap (confirm profile with distributor). Clip: Ipe Clip Extreme (316 SS) or Maximo-specified equivalent, routed groove in board edge, no face fasteners. Clip spacing: 12–16″ o.c. per manufacturer. Fasteners: 316 SS only — no zinc, no chrome, no 304 SS. Framing: PT 2×4 perimeter ledger + PT 2×2 furring at 16″ o.c. Ventilation gap: min. 3/4″ between back face of boards and substrate. Finish: unfinished = silver-gray patina (no maintenance), OR oil finish (Rubio Monocoat or Sansin ENV, UV-stable) = maintains warm honey-brown; decide before installation. Board removal: slide laterally to disengage clip, no tools required, boards fully reusable. |
| Standards     | ASTM A276/A240 (316 SS specification) → see §10 |
| Status        | [TBD] — Soffit finish decision pending. Distributor quote needed. |

> **Rationale:** Hidden 316 SS clips provide full board-by-board maintenance access — any individual board can slide out and be replaced without tools, demolition, or damage to adjacent boards. Brad nails rejected: permanent connection, Thermo Radiata's denser structure splits on pry-out, steel brads rust and streak in salt air. Face screws rejected: visible fasteners break the clean look. Glue rejected: impossible to remove for maintenance. Thermo Radiata selected for dimensional stability and natural decay resistance without chemical treatment. Alternatives rejected: redwood (increasingly expensive, environmentally restricted); Western Red Cedar (less dimensionally stable); Ipe (very heavy, hard to work, expensive, tropical sourcing concerns); painted pine (would fail within a few years in coastal exposure).

> **Rationale:** A parapet provides three functions: (1) screens the roof membrane and drain from street view, maintaining the original stone-facade character; (2) provides supplemental wind-driven rain protection at membrane edges; (3) creates a defined perimeter for the coping cap waterproofing system. As new structural elements in Seismic Design Category D, they require engineered connection to the roof diaphragm with SE stamped drawings.

### §3.F · Mitered Porcelain Coping

| Attribute     | Value |
|---------------|-------|
| Description   | Same porcelain tile series as walls. Sloped INWARD toward roof. Mitered at corners for monolithic appearance. |
| Key Specs     | Material: same porcelain series as wall tile, 2″ mitered pieces cut at 45° from large-format tiles. Slope: INWARD toward roof, min. ¼″:12″ — water drains to roof surface, not exterior wall. Outer drip edge: overhangs exterior parapet face ¾″–1″, underside back-cut near outer edge to break capillary path. Inner edge: flush with or slightly past interior parapet face to sheet water onto roof. Setting mortar: epoxy mortar or ANSI A118.4 polymer-modified thinset + latex additive. Grout: epoxy, non-porous, freeze-thaw resistant. Corner miters: 45° laser-guided cuts, dry-fit and number all pieces, no gap >1/16″ at corners. Movement joints: marine silicone (color-matched) max 8–10 ft o.c. on runs; at every corner; at base of coping against wall tile. Flashing under coping: 26-ga. black Kynar-coated aluminum base flashing under coping mortar bed, turned up on both faces, integrates with ASTM D1970 underlayment — the flashing is the waterproofing, the coping is the finish. |
| Standards     | ANSI A108, ANSI A118.4, ASTM C920 Class 50, ASTM D1970, TCNA EJ171 → see §10 |
| Status        | [TBD] — Pending tile selection and parapet framing completion |

> **Rationale:** Coping must slope toward the roof (inward) to direct rain to the drainage system rather than down the exterior wall face. The miter cut at corners creates a seamless, monolithic appearance that reads as a solid stone cap from street level — consistent with the original architecture's character. Mitered porcelain conceals all metal flashings and waterproofing from view. This is the highest-skill tile task on the project. Alternatives rejected: metal coping (utilitarian, inconsistent with stone character); bullnose tile cap (visible horizontal joint collects dirt and moisture); precast concrete coping (heavy, less precise at corners).
