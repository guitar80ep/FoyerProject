### §3.A · Low-Porosity Porcelain Tile Cladding

| Attribute     | Value |
|---------------|-------|
| Description   | Replace failed travertine with rectified porcelain tile on all wall surfaces and parapet faces |
| Key Specs     | Tile: rectified porcelain, full-body, ≤0.5% absorption (ANSI A137.1 Group V, Impervious), PEI IV or V, matte/honed finish, frost-resistant (ANSI A137.1 F-rated). Format: 24″×24″ or 24″×48″ walls; 12″×24″ planters. Style: travertine-look, warm buff/ivory/gray. Thinset: ANSI A118.4 polymer-modified, 95% min. back-butter coverage. Grout: epoxy, non-porous, salt-resistant; sanded ≥1/8″ joints, unsanded <1/8″. Movement joints: TCNA EJ171, color-matched marine silicone at inside corners, wall-to-floor transitions, max 12 ft o.c. |
| Standards     | ANSI A108, ANSI A118.4, ANSI A137.1, ASTM C920 Class 50 (silicone), TCNA EJ171 → see §10 |
| Status        | [TBD] — Specific tile style/color pending selection and Planning Dept. Condition 23 confirmation |

> **Rationale:** Porcelain at ≤0.5% absorption eliminates the fundamental porosity failure of travertine. Visually identical to natural stone in modern large-format tiles. Sealing not required on tile face (grout joints still need sealer). Available in travertine-look finishes that satisfy Planning Condition 23 warm stone aesthetics. Alternatives rejected: sealed natural stone (sealing degrades in coastal UV/salt); ceramic tile (lower durability, higher absorption); stucco (different aesthetic, not consistent with original approval).

> **Rationale (epoxy grout):** Epoxy grout is non-porous and impervious to salt water. Standard Portland-based grout absorbs moisture and salt ions, leading to spalling and cracking within a few years at coastal locations. Working time is approximately 20–30 minutes at typical SoCal temperatures — mix only what can be placed and cleaned in that window. Remove from tile face before it skins over. Alternatives rejected: unsanded cementitious (absorptive, salt-vulnerable); polymer-modified cementitious (acceptable but still absorptive vs. epoxy).

### §3.B · Full Tear-Down, Framing Repair & Wall Assembly Rebuild

| Attribute     | Value |
|---------------|-------|
| Description   | Demo to structural frame. SE assesses first. Replace rotted sheathing, damaged sill plates, wet framing. Rebuild wall assembly: sheathing → WRB → drainage mat → backer board → thinset → tile. 316 SS weep screed at all wall bases. |
| Key Specs     | Sill plates: PT ACQ or CA-B rated lumber. All framing within 6″ of grade/concrete must be PT. Replace any member with visible rot, soft spots, or >25% section loss — full length, no splice repair. Borate treatment (Tim-bor or equiv.) on all retained framing before sheathing. Sheathing: 5/8″ CDX plywood, nailed per CBC shear wall schedule. WRB: Henry Blueskin VP100 or equiv., shingle-lapped min. 6″. Drainage mat: Benjamin Obdyke Slicker Classic, 3/8″ gap. Backer board: HardieBacker ½″ (ASTM C1325), 316 SS screws per manufacturer schedule. Weep screed: 316 SS V-notch, min. 3/4″ ground face, min. 2″ above finished paving, 316 SS fasteners at 6″ o.c. max, 6″ min. laps sealed with flashing tape. |
| Standards     | AWC NDS 2018 (member sizing), CBC 2022 Chapter 16 (SDC D), CBC 2022 Chapter 23 (shear wall schedule), ASTM C1325 (backer board) → see §10 |
| Status        | [PENDING-SE] — Full framing scope cannot be defined until SE report is in hand |

> **Rationale:** The original travertine was set directly against the substrate with no WRB, no drainage mat, and no weep screed. Any water that penetrated the stone had nowhere to go and accumulated against the wood framing. The new wall assembly creates a complete drainage plane: the WRB blocks bulk water, the drainage mat creates an air gap for water to channel downward, and the weep screed provides a defined exit point at the wall base. This is the critical assembly correction that prevents the original failure mode from recurring.

> **Rationale (316 SS requirement):** All exterior metal fasteners, screws, clips, and weep screeds in this project must be 316 stainless steel. 316 SS contains molybdenum which provides superior resistance to chloride (salt) corrosion. 304 SS — the more common grade — pits and corrodes in coastal salt air within 2–5 years. "SS" or "stainless" without a grade specification almost always means 304. Always verify the grade before accepting any stainless steel product for this project.

### §3.C · Polyglass Three-Ply Self-Adhered SBS Roof Membrane, Scuppers & Rain Chain

| Attribute     | Value |
|---------------|-------|
| Description   | Flat deck re-framed to 5″ rise (½″:12″), draining toward street-facing parapet wall. Three-ply Polyglass ADESO self-adhered SBS modified bitumen membrane system. Primary black Kynar aluminum scupper at low point with rain chain. Overflow scupper 2″ above primary inlet for code-required secondary drainage. |
| Key Specs     | **Membrane System (three-ply, all self-adhered, no torch):** (1) Base ply: Polyglass Elastoflex SA V Plus — 100-mil (2.5 mm) SBS, ADESO dual-compound self-adhered, fiberglass mat reinforced, ASTM D6163. Applied directly to primed 5/8″ CDX plywood deck. (2) Interply: Polyglass Elastoflex SA V — 80-mil SBS, ADESO self-adhered, fiberglass mat reinforced, ASTM D6163. Full coverage over base ply. (3) Cap sheet: Polyglass Polyfresko G SBS SA — 142-mil (3.6 mm) SBS, ADESO self-adhered, polyester reinforced, granule-surfaced (white reflective, CURE Technology), ASTM D6164. SEALLap ULTRA granule-free side laps, FASTLap granule-free end laps. Total system thickness: ~320 mils. **Substrate:** 5/8″ CDX plywood deck, primed with Polyglass Polytack SA primer where required per Polyglass technical guide. Deck must be clean, dry, and above 40°F at time of application. **Laps:** Side laps min. 3″, end laps min. 6″, staggered between plies. Cut 45° angles at T-joints per Polyglass installation guide. **Slope:** ½″:12″ (5″ rise over 122″ run). Building/interior side HIGH, street side LOW. All water drains to primary scupper at street-facing parapet. **Primary Scupper:** 26-ga. black Kynar-coated aluminum, through-wall at street-facing parapet base, set at roof low point. Sized for 97 sq ft at 2″/hr design rainfall (~0.023 cu ft/sec peak). Scupper opening min. 4″ wide × 2″ tall. Integrated spout/downspout adapter for rain chain attachment. Scupper pan extends min. 8″ onto roof surface, fully integrated under membrane base ply. **Overflow Scupper (code-required):** 26-ga. black Kynar-coated aluminum, through-wall at street-facing parapet, set 2″ above primary scupper inlet elevation. Same width as primary or wider. Must drain to a visible location (daylight to exterior wall face). No rain chain connection — overflow drains freely to indicate primary blockage. **Rain Chain:** Black Kynar-coated aluminum cup-style chain, attached to primary scupper spout. Retention clip at scupper outlet to secure against wind. Terminates at rock garden bed (min. 2 ft × 2 ft, 6–8″ deep, ½″–¾″ river rock over filter fabric). **Parapet Base Flashing:** Membrane base ply turns up parapet face min. 8″. Interply and cap sheet terminate at base of parapet turn-up. 26-ga. black Kynar-coated aluminum two-piece counter/base flashing at roof-to-parapet transition, integrated with membrane. **Future Maintenance — Silicone Recoat:** At year 10–15, apply Polyglass PolyBrite 90.1 or 95.1 silicone roof coating over cap sheet per manufacturer specifications. Extends membrane service life 10–15 additional years per recoat cycle. Confirm cap sheet surface compatibility with PolyBrite primer requirements before first recoat. **Cool Roof:** Polyfresko G SBS SA cap sheet meets cool roof standards (SRI initial: 96, aged: 82). Confirms Title 24 cool-roof compliance if required. |
| Standards     | ASTM D6163 (SBS base/interply), ASTM D6164 (SBS cap sheet), CBC 2022 §1503.4 (primary roof drainage), CBC 2022 §1503.4.2 (overflow/secondary drainage), ASTM D1970 (reference — membrane exceeds this spec) → see §10. **Manufacturer references (installation only):** Polyglass ADESO Low-Slope Installation Guide; Polyglass Polytack SA primer technical data sheet; Polyglass PolyBrite 90.1/95.1 silicone coating technical data sheet. |
| Status        | [TBD] — Confirm Polyglass product availability and lead time with supplier. Scupper sizing to be field-verified after demo. |

> **Rationale (system change from standing seam metal):** The original specification (McClelroy Maxima 3″ structural standing seam) delivered the longest raw service life (40–50 years) but required a rented mechanical seamer, manufacturer-specific clips with FM/UL confirmation, a 10-item pre-order checklist with McElroy rep coordination, and precise seam engagement technique — all factors that increase DIY error risk on a first-time installation. The Polyglass three-ply self-adhered SBS system provides a ~320-mil fully bonded membrane with 20–30 year base life, extendable to 30–40+ years with periodic silicone recoat (PolyBrite 90.1/95.1, ~half-day job on 97 sq ft every 10–15 years). Installation is peel-and-stick with no torch, no hot asphalt, no mechanical seamer, and no specialized equipment beyond a roller. The owner's main roof already uses the Polyglass ADESO product family (Elastobase V, iElastoflex SA V, Polyfresko SA), so the material handling is familiar, the supplier relationship exists, and the single-manufacturer membrane + coating warranty path is available. Alternatives rejected: McClelroy Maxima standing seam (excessive DIY complexity, specialized equipment, long lead time); EPDM rubber (excellent DIY option but no existing supplier relationship, and granule-surfaced SBS provides better puncture and UV resistance); TPO/PVC (hot-air welding required for reliable seams, higher DIY complexity); liquid-applied membrane (shortest lifespan, 10–15 years standalone, film thickness control difficult for DIY).

> **Rationale (slope direction):** Minimum code slope is ¼″:12″ (~2.5″ rise over 122″). Owner elected 5″ (½″:12″) for margin. Draining toward street puts the rain chain at the most visible, accessible location — the street-facing entry — as a design feature. Building side being higher means debris-blockage ponding stays away from the building wall.

> **Rationale (scupper + rain chain vs. gutter):** Eliminating the gutter removes ~230″ of half-round gutter, ~24–28 hangers, and the gutter slope/alignment task. A through-wall scupper is a simpler, more reliable primary drain for a parapet-enclosed roof — water exits at the parapet base rather than overtopping an edge into a gutter trough. The rain chain connects directly to the scupper spout, which is a cleaner architectural detail than a gutter outlet adapter. Black Kynar aluminum scupper + rain chain retains the original design intent of making drainage visible as a feature.

> **Rationale (overflow scupper — code requirement):** CBC 2022 §1503.4.2 requires secondary (overflow) drainage for roof areas enclosed by parapets. The foyer roof is a contained basin on all four sides — if the primary scupper is blocked by debris, water has no exit path and imposes increasing structural load. The overflow scupper is set 2″ above the primary inlet so it only activates when the primary is blocked. It drains freely (no rain chain) to a visible exterior location so active flow is an unmistakable signal that the primary needs clearing. This is a code requirement for permit approval, not optional.

> **Rationale (black Kynar aluminum):** All drainage and flashing components use 26-ga. Kynar 500 / PVDF-coated aluminum in black. Kynar coating provides 30+ year UV and salt-air resistance — the same coating chemistry proven on standing seam metal roofs in coastal environments. Black color creates a cohesive, recessive visual against the warm porcelain tile and parapet, letting the tile be the dominant material at street level. Aluminum is lighter and easier to cut, bend, and fabricate on site than copper — better suited to DIY. Alternatives rejected: copper (higher material cost, requires soldering skill for watertight joints, patina color may not complement all tile selections, heavier); galvanized steel (corrodes in coastal salt air within 5–10 years); painted steel (paint degrades, requires maintenance recoating); 316 SS (expensive, difficult to form/bend on site with hand tools).

### §3.D · Maximo Thermo Radiata Soffit

| Attribute     | Value |
|---------------|-------|
| Description   | Thermally modified radiata pine on archway soffit. 316 SS hidden clip system. PT framing with 316 SS fasteners. |
| Key Specs     | Board: Maximo Thermo Radiata pine, 5/4″ or 1″ nominal T&G or shiplap (confirm profile with distributor). Clip: Ipe Clip Extreme (316 SS) or Maximo-specified equivalent, routed groove in board edge, no face fasteners. Clip spacing: 12–16″ o.c. per manufacturer. Fasteners: 316 SS only — no zinc, no chrome, no 304 SS. Framing: PT 2×4 perimeter ledger + PT 2×2 furring at 16″ o.c. Ventilation gap: min. 3/4″ between back face of boards and substrate. Finish: unfinished = silver-gray patina (no maintenance), OR oil finish (Rubio Monocoat or Sansin ENV, UV-stable) = maintains warm honey-brown; decide before installation. Board removal: slide laterally to disengage clip, no tools required, boards fully reusable. |
| Standards     | ASTM A276/A240 (316 SS specification) → see §10 |
| Status        | [TBD] — Soffit finish decision pending. Distributor quote needed. |

> **Rationale:** Hidden 316 SS clips provide full board-by-board maintenance access — any individual board can slide out and be replaced without tools, demolition, or damage to adjacent boards. Brad nails rejected: permanent connection, Thermo Radiata's denser structure splits on pry-out, steel brads rust and streak in salt air. Face screws rejected: visible fasteners break the clean look. Glue rejected: impossible to remove for maintenance. Thermo Radiata selected for dimensional stability and natural decay resistance without chemical treatment. Alternatives rejected: redwood (increasingly expensive, environmentally restricted); Western Red Cedar (less dimensionally stable); Ipe (very heavy, hard to work, expensive, tropical sourcing concerns); painted pine (would fail within a few years in coastal exposure).

### §3.E · New 8″ Wood-Framed Parapet — All Sides

| Attribute     | Value |
|---------------|-------|
| Description   | Entirely new structure (no existing parapet). 8″ tall wood-framed parapet on all four sides. Street-side parapet screens scuppers and roof membrane from view. |
| Key Specs     | Studs: 2×6 or 2×8 at 16″ o.c. per SE specification. Connectors: Simpson Strong-Tie LTP4 or SE-specified, connecting to roof diaphragm. Sheathing: 5/8″ CDX plywood/OSB. WRB: self-adhering, wrapped from roof side continuously over parapet top and 4″ down exterior face. Base flashing: continuous metal at roof-to-parapet transition, turned up min. 8″ on parapet interior face. Exterior face: porcelain tile cladding per §3.A. Coping: mitered porcelain per §3.F. Corners: blocking and metal strap ties for continuous load path. Top elevation: must be at a single consistent elevation around all four sides (critical for coping miters and water drainage). Street-facing parapet: two through-wall penetrations for primary and overflow scuppers → see §3.C. Scupper openings framed with PT blocking, flashed with 26-ga. black Kynar aluminum pans integrated into membrane and WRB. |
| Standards     | ASCE 7-22 (wind and seismic load basis), AWC NDS 2018 (member sizing), CBC 2022 Chapter 16 (SDC D seismic provisions), CBC 2022 Chapter 23 (wood framing), SE stamped drawings required → see §10 |
| Status        | [PENDING-SE] — SE to specify member sizes, connector types, and quantities |

> **Rationale:** A parapet provides three functions: (1) screens the roof membrane and scuppers from street view, maintaining the original stone-facade character; (2) provides supplemental wind-driven rain protection at membrane edges; (3) creates a defined perimeter for the coping cap waterproofing system. As new structural elements in Seismic Design Category D, they require engineered connection to the roof diaphragm with SE stamped drawings.

### §3.F · Mitered Porcelain Coping

| Attribute     | Value |
|---------------|-------|
| Description   | Same porcelain tile series as walls. Sloped INWARD toward roof. Mitered at corners for monolithic appearance. |
| Key Specs     | Material: same porcelain series as wall tile, 2″ mitered pieces cut at 45° from large-format tiles. Slope: INWARD toward roof, min. ¼″:12″ — water drains to roof surface, not exterior wall. Outer drip edge: overhangs exterior parapet face ¾″–1″, underside back-cut near outer edge to break capillary path. Inner edge: flush with or slightly past interior parapet face to sheet water onto roof. Setting mortar: epoxy mortar or ANSI A118.4 polymer-modified thinset + latex additive. Grout: epoxy, non-porous, freeze-thaw resistant. Corner miters: 45° laser-guided cuts, dry-fit and number all pieces, no gap >1/16″ at corners. Movement joints: marine silicone (color-matched) max 8–10 ft o.c. on runs; at every corner; at base of coping against wall tile. Flashing under coping: 26-ga. black Kynar-coated aluminum base flashing under coping mortar bed, turned up on both faces, integrates with ASTM D1970 underlayment — the flashing is the waterproofing, the coping is the finish. |
| Standards     | ANSI A108, ANSI A118.4, ASTM C920 Class 50, ASTM D1970, TCNA EJ171 → see §10 |
| Status        | [TBD] — Pending tile selection and parapet framing completion |

> **Rationale:** Coping must slope toward the roof (inward) to direct rain to the drainage system rather than down the exterior wall face. The miter cut at corners creates a seamless, monolithic appearance that reads as a solid stone cap from street level — consistent with the original architecture's character. Mitered porcelain conceals all metal flashings and waterproofing from view. This is the highest-skill tile task on the project. Alternatives rejected: metal coping (utilitarian, inconsistent with stone character); bullnose tile cap (visible horizontal joint collects dirt and moisture); precast concrete coping (heavy, less precise at corners).

| Status        | [TBD] — Pending confirmation of homeowner permit pathway vs. C-10 requirement |
