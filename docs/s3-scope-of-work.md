### §3.A · Low-Porosity Porcelain Tile Cladding

| Attribute     | Value |
|---------------|-------|
| Description   | Replace failed travertine with rectified porcelain tile on all wall surfaces and parapet faces |
| Key Specs     | Tile: rectified porcelain, full-body, ≤0.5% absorption (ANSI A137.1 Group V, Impervious), PEI IV or V, matte/honed finish, frost-resistant (ANSI A137.1 F-rated). Format: 24″×24″ or 24″×48″ walls; 12″×24″ planters. Style: travertine-look, warm buff/ivory/gray. Thinset: ANSI A118.4 polymer-modified, 95% min. back-butter coverage. Grout: epoxy, non-porous, salt-resistant; sanded ≥1/8″ joints, unsanded <1/8″. Movement joints: TCNA EJ171, color-matched marine silicone at inside corners, wall-to-floor transitions, max 12 ft o.c. |
| Standards     | ANSI A108, ANSI A118.4, ANSI A137.1, ASTM C920 Class 50 (silicone), TCNA EJ171 → see §10 |
| Status        | [TBD] — Specific tile style/color pending selection and Planning Dept. Condition 23 confirmation |

> **Rationale:** Porcelain at ≤0.5% absorption eliminates the fundamental porosity failure of travertine. Visually identical to natural stone in modern large-format tiles. Sealing not required on tile face (grout joints still need sealer). Available in travertine-look finishes that satisfy Planning Condition 23 warm stone aesthetics. Alternatives rejected: sealed natural stone (sealing degrades in coastal UV/salt); ceramic tile (lower durability, higher absorption); stucco (different aesthetic, not consistent with original approval).

> **Rationale (epoxy grout):** Epoxy grout is non-porous and impervious to salt water. Standard Portland-based grout absorbs moisture and salt ions, leading to spalling and cracking within a few years at coastal locations. Working time is approximately 20–30 minutes at typical SoCal temperatures — mix only what can be placed and cleaned in that window. Remove from tile face before it skins over. Alternatives rejected: unsanded cementitious (absorptive, salt-vulnerable); polymer-modified cementitious (acceptable but still absorptive vs. epoxy).

### §3.B · Full Tear-Down, Framing Repair & Wall Assembly Rebuild

| Attribute     | Value |
|---------------|-------|
| Description   | Demo to structural frame. SE assesses first. Replace rotted sheathing, damaged sill plates, wet framing. Rebuild wall assembly: sheathing → WRB → drainage mat → backer board → thinset → tile. 316 SS weep screed at all wall bases. |
| Key Specs     | Sill plates: PT ACQ or CA-B rated lumber. All framing within 6″ of grade/concrete must be PT. Replace any member with visible rot, soft spots, or >25% section loss — full length, no splice repair. Borate treatment (Tim-bor or equiv.) on all retained framing before sheathing. Sheathing: 5/8″ CDX plywood, nailed per CBC shear wall schedule. WRB: Henry Blueskin VP100 or equiv., shingle-lapped min. 6″. Drainage mat: Benjamin Obdyke Slicker Classic, 3/8″ gap. Backer board: HardieBacker ½″ (ASTM C1325), 316 SS screws per manufacturer schedule. Weep screed: 316 SS V-notch, min. 3/4″ ground face, min. 2″ above finished paving, 316 SS fasteners at 6″ o.c. max, 6″ min. laps sealed with flashing tape. |
| Standards     | AWC NDS 2018 (member sizing), CBC 2022 Chapter 16 (SDC D), CBC 2022 Chapter 23 (shear wall schedule), ASTM C1325 (backer board) → see §10 |
| Status        | [PENDING-SE] — Full framing scope cannot be defined until SE report is in hand |

> **Rationale:** The original travertine was set directly against the substrate with no WRB, no drainage mat, and no weep screed. Any water that penetrated the stone had nowhere to go and accumulated against the wood framing. The new wall assembly creates a complete drainage plane: the WRB blocks bulk water, the drainage mat creates an air gap for water to channel downward, and the weep screed provides a defined exit point at the wall base. This is the critical assembly correction that prevents the original failure mode from recurring.

> **Rationale (316 SS requirement):** All exterior metal fasteners, screws, clips, and weep screeds in this project must be 316 stainless steel. 316 SS contains molybdenum which provides superior resistance to chloride (salt) corrosion. 304 SS — the more common grade — pits and corrodes in coastal salt air within 2–5 years. "SS" or "stainless" without a grade specification almost always means 304. Always verify the grade before accepting any stainless steel product for this project.

> **Rationale:** Structural standing seam is the highest-performing low-slope roofing system available. The 3″ rib height channels water efficiently even at minimal slope. Hidden clip fasteners eliminate penetrations through the panel face — zero exposed fasteners in the roof field. Kynar 500 coating is rated for 30+ years in coastal environments (standard polyester begins chalking within 5–10 years in salt air). The original flat tiled roof had no proper membrane beneath it; tile joints on a low-slope roof are not waterproof. Alternatives rejected: modified bitumen membrane (requires replacement every 15–20 years); TPO/EPDM (less elegant, visible seams); barrel tile (too heavy for existing framing, wrong aesthetic).

> **Rationale (slope direction):** Minimum code slope is ¼″:12″ (~2.5″ rise over 122″). Owner elected 5″ (½″:12″) for margin. Draining toward Morgan Lane puts the gutter and rain chain on the street-facing entry side. The gutter is hidden behind the Morgan Lane parapet — not visible from the street — while the rain chain at the Blossom Lane corner serves as a visible architectural feature. Alternatives rejected: slope toward building (gutter less accessible, pools water against building); center drain (requires roof penetration, adds leak risk).

> **Rationale (gutter position — inside parapet):** Positioning the gutter inside the Morgan Lane parapet completely screens it from street view. The roof deck drains directly into the gutter at the base of the parapet interior face — no fascia bracket, no external gutter profile visible from Morgan Lane. The parapet provides both visual screening and physical protection of the gutter from wind-driven debris.

> **Rationale (scupper on low/street side):** The overflow scupper must be on the same side as the gutter (Morgan Lane parapet, low side). A scupper on the high/building-side parapet cannot activate until water ponds across the full roof and rises to the high-side parapet height — requiring several inches of standing water with no practical overflow protection. On the low side at 2″ above gutter inlet, the scupper activates with minimal ponding depth.

> **Rationale (aluminum Kynar system — gutter, rain chain, and flashings):** All metal components in the drainage and flashing system use aluminum as the base metal, eliminating galvanic corrosion risk by design rather than relying on coating integrity or isolation hardware. Kynar 500 (PVDF 70% resin minimum) provides 30+ year coastal performance on the panels — the same coating on the gutter and flashings creates a single unified material system. Alternatives rejected: copper (premium cost, galvanic risk at interfaces, patina aesthetic no longer the design driver); 316 SS mixed with aluminum (galvanic couple in salt-air environment even with isolation; coating breach during field fabrication creates corrosion risk). K-style profile selected over half-round to match existing house gutters and provide a native fit for the Monarch Abode triangular rain chain clip without adapter. Matte Black color selected to contrast with Antique White / Classic Ivory panels — the black gutter and chain read as intentional design accent at the entry rather than a continuation of the roof color. Black powder-coat on the rain chain is acceptable for a decorative element at this price point — it is replaceable if finish degrades, whereas the base aluminum substrate will not corrode.

> **Rationale (4″ K-style gutter sizing):** 97 sq ft roof area at 2″/hr design rain intensity produces ~0.023 cu ft/sec peak flow. 4″ K-style handles this load with margin. Profile matches existing house gutters for visual consistency. Alternatives rejected: 3″ half-round (specialty profile, incompatible with standard rain chain clip, harder to source in Kynar 500); 5″ K-style (oversized for this run, heavier, unnecessary).

> **Rationale:** A parapet provides three functions: (1) screens the standing seam panels and gutter from street view, maintaining the original stone-facade character; (2) provides supplemental wind-driven rain protection at panel seams; (3) creates a defined perimeter for the coping cap waterproofing system. As new structural elements in Seismic Design Category D, they require engineered connection to the roof diaphragm with SE stamped drawings.

> **Rationale (interior face — liner not tile):** The parapet interior face is not visible from street level or any public vantage point. Tiling it requires backer board, thinset, epoxy grout, and movement joints on a short 8″ vertical run — significant cost and skill for a hidden surface. An aluminum Kynar 500 flat sheet liner in the same color as the roof panels is fully weathertight, integrates cleanly with the all-aluminum Kynar flashing system per §3.H with no dissimilar metal contact, requires no substrate preparation beyond the D1970 underlayment, and will outlast tile on this moisture-exposed surface. Alternatives rejected: tile (overkill for hidden surface, more joints to fail); fiber cement + paint (paint degrades in coastal UV/salt air); exposed D1970 (UV-vulnerable without protection).

> **Rationale (liner as slip-fit):** The liner top tab tucks behind the coping flashing interior turn-down and the bottom tab laps over the base flashing horizontal leg — neither end is fastened or sealed. This allows independent thermal movement of all three layers (base flashing, counter-flashing, liner) without breaking any seal, and makes the liner fully removable for inspection of the flashings behind it.

> **Rationale (two-piece base/counter flashing):** Base and counter flashings are independent pieces so thermal movement does not stress the waterproofing seal. The base flashing is the primary seal — it integrates with D1970 and is permanently fastened. The counter flashing is a protective lap that shields the top of the base flashing from direct water entry. If sealed together, differential movement between the parapet framing and roof deck would eventually crack the joint.

### §3.H · Aluminum Kynar Flashing System

| Attribute     | Value |
|---------------|-------|
| Description   | Complete flashing system for parapet base, counter-flashing, and coping. All-aluminum Kynar 500 construction — no dissimilar metals. |
| Key Specs     | **Materials:** Base flashing and counter-flashing: 0.032″ aluminum sheet, Kynar 500 / PVDF 70% resin min. coating, Antique White / Classic Ivory (color-matched to panels). Coping flashing: 0.024″ aluminum sheet, Kynar 500, Antique White / Classic Ivory. All aluminum alloy 3003-H14 or 3105-H14 — standard brake-forming grades. **Cut edge treatment:** Every field-cut edge must be treated with compatible Kynar touch-up paint or aluminum edge sealant before installation. Bare aluminum exposed at cut edges will oxidize in coastal salt air — this step is mandatory, not optional. **Fastener schedule:** Aluminum blind rivets (aluminum mandrel, 3/16″ diameter, ≥3/8″ washer face) at all lap joints, 6″ o.c. Aluminum screws (6061 alloy or equiv., #10 × 1-1/2″ min.) with neoprene bonded washers at all substrate attachments, spacing per SE wind uplift confirmation — preliminary 6″ o.c. at base flashing, 8″ o.c. at coping flashing. No 316 SS or any other non-aluminum fastener anywhere in the flashing system. **Lap joints:** Side laps min. 4″. End laps min. 6″. Continuous bead of ASTM C920 Class 25 polyurethane sealant in every lap before fastening — sealant is the primary water seal, mechanical fastener is the clamp. **Sealant spec:** ASTM C920 Class 25 polyurethane (e.g., Tremco Dymonic or equiv.) at all laps, penetrations, and transitions. ASTM C920 Class 50 marine silicone (color-matched) at all exposed terminations and movement joints. Do not mix polyurethane and silicone in the same joint. **Corner treatment:** Inside corners: pre-formed aluminum Kynar inside corner units — do not field-form inside corners, they are the highest-leak-risk location on the parapet. Outside corners: field-formed with tight miter, sealed hem, and continuous sealant bead at joint. **Integration sequence with ASTM D1970, WRB, and liner:** (1) Install ASTM D1970 self-adhering underlayment over full roof deck, turn up full parapet interior face height; (2) Install base flashing at roof-to-parapet transition — vertical leg up min. 8″ on parapet interior face over D1970, horizontal leg over D1970 on deck min. 4″; (3) Install counter-flashing into reglet, lapping over base flashing min. 4″ — do not seal to base flashing; (4) Install WRB on parapet exterior face, lapping over counter-flashing top by min. 4″; (5) Install coping flashing — under mortar bed, exterior turn-down min. 2½″, interior turn-down min. 3″, integrated with WRB; (6) Install liner per §3.E — top tab tucks behind coping flashing interior turn-down, bottom tab laps over base flashing horizontal leg. **Penetration sealing:** Any conduit, fastener, or pipe penetrating the flashing plane must be sealed with ASTM C920 polyurethane applied to back of penetration before driving fastener, and finished with surface sealant bead around penetration perimeter on face side. **SE confirmation required:** SE to confirm base flashing fastener pull-out spec meets wind uplift requirements for coastal Exposure Category at this parapet geometry before finalizing fastener schedule. Preliminary 6″ o.c. aluminum screw schedule may need tightening to 4″ o.c. — do not build until SE confirms. |
| Standards     | SMACNA Architectural Sheet Metal Manual 7th Ed. (flashing geometry, lap lengths, corner treatment, dissimilar metal guidance), ASTM C920 Class 25 (polyurethane sealant), ASTM C920 Class 50 (marine silicone), ASTM D1970 (underlayment integration) → see §10 |
| Status        | [PENDING-SE] — Fastener pull-out spec and final fastener schedule require SE confirmation before fabrication |

> **Rationale:** All-aluminum construction eliminates galvanic corrosion risk by design — no dissimilar metal contact anywhere in the flashing system, removing dependency on coating integrity or isolation hardware to prevent electrochemical degradation. Kynar 500 coating on aluminum provides the same 30+ year coastal performance as the roof panels. 0.032″ gauge for base and counter-flashing provides sufficient rigidity for field forming and wind resistance at the parapet face; 0.024″ for coping flashing is appropriate as it is fully supported by the mortar bed and not subject to wind load. Pre-formed inside corners are mandatory — field-formed inside corners are the most common source of parapet flashing leaks and are not acceptable at this installation. ASTM C920 polyurethane at laps provides adhesive bond in addition to water seal, superior to sealant-free lapped joints. Alternatives rejected: copper flashing (cost premium, patina aesthetic no longer the design driver, galvanic risk at interfaces with aluminum gutter); 316 SS flashing (galvanic couple with aluminum system even with isolation; coating breach during field fabrication creates localized corrosion risk); aluminum without Kynar coating (bare aluminum oxidizes and pits in coastal salt air within 5–10 years).
